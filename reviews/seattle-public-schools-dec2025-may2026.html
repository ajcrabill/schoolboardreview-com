About This Review

This is a transcript-based governance review, covering Seattle Public Schools' board meetings from December 2025 through May 2026. Unlike reviews based on meeting minutes, this review draws on verbatim transcripts of all 14 meetings in the period — sourced from transcripts.sps-by-the-numbers.com, a third-party archive of SPS board meeting transcripts — enabling direct citation of what board members said and how they engaged with information.

The 20-question rubric assesses board behavior across five governance dimensions. Every scored point is cited to a specific meeting, speaker, and quote. Near-misses — evidence that was relevant but failed one of the question's criteria — are documented to demonstrate where the 0.8 confidence gate was applied.

SPS has a meaningful governance infrastructure: formally adopted student outcome goals (Policy 0020, January 2025), a guardrails framework, and Policy 1010 requiring a monitoring calendar. This review documents the gap between that architecture and actual board practice.

What the Board Does Well

When called to engage with outcome data, this board is genuinely capable. The three monitoring sessions from December 2025 through February 2026 show a board that asks hard questions, accepts negative findings without minimization, and pushes past headline metrics to understand what the data actually reveals.

The January 14 Life Ready monitoring session is the clearest example. Staff presented a headline graduation rate of 84.8% — a number that would satisfy most boards. The SPS board did not stop there. Directors pressed on what "life ready" actually means in practice and surfaced a critical gap: only 4.6% of 11th graders had completed all four required High School and Beyond Plan activities.

"Directors questioned whether graduation rates accurately reflect student preparedness, noting that students admitted to Seattle Promise and other colleges often require remedial coursework."

January 14, 2026 — Board directors during Life Ready monitoring session

Student board members reinforced the point with ground-level evidence — one noting they used Excel rather than the district's official Naviance platform, another describing one-hour bus rides to access CTE programs. The board did not dismiss this inconsistency; it registered as a gap between what program-access data implied and what students actually experienced.

The February 4 mathematics monitoring session showed similar quality. When the math data revealed growth insufficient to close achievement gaps, Director Briggs named the implication directly rather than accepting the staff framing.

"Targets maintain rather than shrink gaps."

February 4, 2026 — Vice President Briggs during math monitoring session

That is the kind of observation that separates governance from rubber-stamping. The board also demonstrated honest engagement with declining data: multilingual learner math proficiency showing a declining trajectory across grade progression was presented without minimization and engaged with substantively. The December 3 monitoring session produced explicit requests for disaggregated data by IEP status and Native American students as separate categories — active monitoring behavior, not passive receipt.

The board's governance architecture is also genuinely strong. Three student outcome goals formally adopted by board action, with metrics and timelines, is more than most districts achieve. The April 29 goals deliberation showed directors thinking carefully about how to make the goals more operationally useful — Director Rankin proposing interim check-ins, Director LaVallee requesting a regular reporting cadence tied to actionable plans.

Where Governance Breaks Down

Issue 1 — Monitoring Exists Only in Special Sessions All three dedicated monitoring sessions in this review period were specially called meetings. Not one regular board meeting in six months included a standing outcome monitoring item. When budget pressures, immigration controversies, curriculum debates, and a superintendent transition filled the regular meeting calendar, outcome monitoring simply did not happen. The board went from the Feb 4 monitoring session — the sharpest data engagement in the review period — to zero monitoring across eight straight meetings (Feb 11 through May 13). A board that monitors outcomes only when it creates a special meeting to do so has not institutionalized monitoring; it has approximated it.
Issue 2 — The Guardrails Framework Is Losing Its Teeth SPS adopted five guardrails in January 2025 using "the superintendent shall not" framing — the standard language for a governance constraint. On April 29, 2026, Superintendent Shuldiner proposed reframing them to "the district led by the superintendent shall work towards" language. Director Briggs raised the core problem directly: it becomes impossible to evaluate compliance with aspirational language. As of the review period, the board had not resolved this dispute. If the reframe proceeds without a measurability requirement, the guardrails — currently scoring at 3/5 — will become functionally unenforceable.
Issue 3 — Budget Decisions Are Disconnected from Goals In a district managing a structural deficit of roughly $100 million, budget decisions were the most consequential governance work of the review period. Yet in 13 of 14 meetings, budget discussions contained no explicit connection between specific allocations and the three named student outcome goals. The January 28 budget work session was the exception — Superintendent Podesta explicitly framed the budget as "a funding plan to achieve our goals" and mapped investments to all three goals.

"The budget is a funding plan to achieve our goals, not just, oh, $80 million short, what are we gonna do about that?"

January 28, 2026 — Superintendent Podesta during budget work session

That framing did not carry forward. March and May budget sessions addressed the deficit as a fiscal problem, not a goal-alignment problem. Of five qualifying major expenditures in the review period, only the ELA curriculum adoption had an explicit goal connection in deliberation.
Issue 4 — Community Has No Regular Window Into Goal Progress SPS has three formally adopted student outcome goals. As of the close of this review period, no regular structured mechanism existed for the public to track progress against those goals. On April 29, Director LaVallee requested a quarterly reporting cadence; on May 6, over 1,000 community members engaged in budget sessions. But the budget sessions were about resource decisions, not goal progress. The public currently has access to goal monitoring only by attending the same special sessions the board attends — and those special sessions stopped after February.

The Time Use Picture

Transcript-based time use analysis across all 14 meetings reveals a board whose agenda is dominated by operational content. Of 1,775 total public meeting minutes in the review period, only 300 minutes — 16.9% — were goal-focused (Goal Monitoring + Goal Setting combined). The ESB benchmark target is 50%.

The more revealing number is the distribution. Goal-focused time was concentrated in three special sessions: Dec 3 (69.6%), Jan 14 (73.9%), Feb 4 (45.8%), and Apr 29 (56.5%). Nine of fourteen meetings had zero goal-focused time. Public testimony (primarily on immigration enforcement, budget cuts, and special education) consumed a disproportionate share of the regular meeting calendar, with no mechanism to ensure goal monitoring persisted through the turbulence.

The top categories in the "Other" bucket — the 66% of meeting time not classified as goal-focused or operational governance — were public testimony, recognition ceremonies, superintendent/staff reports, and immigration protocol discussions. None of these are unimportant. But their dominance of regular meeting time, combined with the total absence of monitoring from the second half of the review period, confirms that SPS's outcome governance is a special-session phenomenon, not an embedded practice.

One Specific Recommendation

Structural Change

The board should vote, before July 2026, on three interlinked actions: (1) adopt a binding monitoring calendar under Policy 1010 that assigns a standing 30-minute "Goal [1/2/3] Status Update" item to every regular board meeting, rotating across goals on a published schedule with a staff memo required 72 hours in advance; (2) vote the revised numerical baselines and targets deliberated April 29 into Policy 0020 as a formal amendment, so the goals meet the Score-5 SMART threshold; and (3) resolve the guardrails reframing dispute in favor of retaining "the superintendent shall not" constraint language paired with measurable compliance indicators, rather than converting guardrails to aspirational framing. These three actions address the root cause of SPS's governance gap: strong written commitments that are not structurally enforced.

Governance Takeaway

What All Boards Can Learn

A board that only monitors student outcomes when it creates a special meeting to do so has not institutionalized outcome governance — the strongest boards understand that architecture is not practice, and that the distance between written goals and actual governance discipline is closed not by intention but by structure: a monitoring calendar that makes oversight inevitable whether or not there is a budget crisis, a superintendent transition, or a community controversy in the same week.
